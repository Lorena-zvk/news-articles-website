Halloween

-and why people love to be scared-

A fright night spook festival, Halloween is a little different than other special holiday seasons we’re used to. The celebration of Halloween has long historical roots that can be traced back to the Celtic celebration of Samhain, as a part of pagan religious belief. Halloween’s origin evolved from Hallowe’en, and over time, the name slowly got shortened down to “hallowed evening”, referring to All Hallows’ Eve, later to Halloween.

Halloween is wildly popular across the countries - unlike the contemporary holiday seasons and is loved by people young and old. Occurs every year on the last day of October - marking the end of summer. The season deliberately exploits a way to experience the fun and fright during the first day of winter.

The second most popular holiday in the US, millions of people around the world gather around on the night of 31st of October, put on a costume and for one night they pretend like our childhood monsters are real.

Holidays like Halloween are deeply rooted in history and folklore and have evolved throughout the centuries. Today, we are far from the once Celtic festival of Samhain when people would dress up to ward off ghosts. Ironically, today we tend to dress up in hopes of finding ghosts.

Every part of the world has its own legends and myths, some more frightening than others, but all equally captivating. In a Celtic folklore, it’s believed spirits are roaming the physical world on this special occasion, the boundaries between worlds are opening for these spirits to come and go as pleased but only on Halloween.

Although the actual practice didn’t require the participant to wear costumes, it had evolved during times and undergone some changes. The purpose was to ward off various supernatural beings because it’s believed that the worlds between the spirit and physical world have been opened during this time. In addition, offering food to the dead was another way for the Celtic to connect with the spirit.

There is a long history of how Halloween came to be, the background is immense with an in-depth on the importance of this holiday. The fright wasn’t necessarily part of Halloween in the past, in fact, it’s a part of pagan religious ritual with building bonfires and celebrating the dead. Surely, Halloween has changed and diverted from its root, transforming the tradition with a twist.

In addition, Halloween emphasizes the celebration of the ghosts and those who have died, it's obvious that this can be linked to the spookiness. The whole Halloween tradition and practice originates from ancient Celtic which is now known as Ireland, Halloween didn’t arrive in the States until the 19th century and has been adopted as an official holiday season since then.

North America/Native American

Skinwalker:

In the Navajo culture, a skinwalker originates from a shaman who practices black magic, and hunger for power has led a shaman into a creature. A skinwalker should not be mentioned because the Navajo peoples believe that once mentioned its name, that skinwalker will come calling. The power a skinwalker possesses is the ability to transform into animals, preferably a creature which resembles a large wolf/dog, but they can shapeshift as well.

Wendigo:

Also known as “windigo”, which has its root from Canadian indigenous peoples (the Algonquin). A wendigo is a cannibalistic malevolent spirit which seeks to possess a human body and goes on a feasting rampage. Because of their insatiable thirst for human flesh, no matter how much flesh they have consumed, it will never be enough. This sounds pretty similar to a skinwalker, however, there’s a vast difference and can be distinguished easily. What a wendigo’s spirit will do is to seek out a human being and possess that body. Undertake the host slowly as it influences the human into greed and hunger, causing the human to slowly undergo a transformation. Wendigo appearance: Tall, usually 7ft (2m + or taller), extremely thin (due to starvation) with bones protruding from its body, the head may change slightly from what a human used to look like.

South America

Yacumama:

Roaming the Amazon River, this ancient sea creature belongs to South-America's folklore, and according to the legend, Yacumama is believed to be the mother of all marine life and regarded as a protective spirit of the Amazon River. Yacumama could definitely be another resemblance of anaconda but larger.

England

Spring-heeled Jack:

Being a part of the Victorian era, Spring-heeled Jack is an entity who has been seen since 1837, his sightings had once been reported all over UK, most specific areas such as suburban London, the Midlands and Scotland.

Black dog:

The creature’s name implies a perfect description of a large canine with glowing red eyes. The folklore stems from British Isles, the black dog is nocturnal and oftentime being said to associate with a Hellhound. It has been said the black dogs are harmful and may also be bringing bad omen. Not much is known about the origin on how the black dog came to be what it is - an apparition, but certain the mystery still continues.

Norway

Nøkken:

Is a terrifying and dangerous creature, inhabitant in rivers, lakes and ponds. Nøkken is a water spirit of a certain type who has the capability to shape shift and take many forms depending upon the victim it has killed. The creature can lure humans into the river by disguising itself either as a horse, or some attractive person.

Huldra:

In folklore, this creature is told countless times that it’s primarily female huldra. A huldra will have long blond hair, with all the facial features similar to a human being except for the tail, she possesses a cow tail. She has been known to seduce young unmarried men and take them into the mountains, only by marrying her will she let the men go.

Romania

Strigoi:

Comes with many different names, a strigoi is Romanian’s vampires which literally are spirits of the dead. Strigoi may look human but their skin is much paler than a normal human being. A dead giveaway for this creature is the fangs.

Solomonari:

Refers to a wizard who rides a dragon and has the power to control weather, causing rain, thunder or hailstorm. The Solomonar is basically selected from common folk and taught them magic. Solomonari’s appearance: Tall, red-haired, wearing long white robes and oftentimes come woolen or in a ragged outfit made from patches.

Thailand

Krasue:

A creature which depicts a young woman with head floating and only attached with the intestines.

Naga:

Sharing between Hinduism and Buddhism, a naga is a large serpent, exists to protect Buddha from becoming astray. These semi divine beings who are half human and half snake. Legend has it that these serpents reside at the bottom of deep rivers, lakes and oceans.

Japan

Jorogumo:

Is the Japanese version of a giant spider, this folklore creature is a spider-like demon. In general, the Jorogumo is evil and hunger for human flesh. To lure their prey, the Jorogumo would disguise themselves as attractive women. Jorogumo’s appearance: Enormous, spider-like with 8 limbs like a spider, can shapeshift into a disguise.

Russia

Baba Yaga:

The name stems from the Slavic languages and consists of two elements, with the Baba referring to “grandmother” or “old woman”. Baba Yaga is a witch who resembles a sweet grandmother. Baba Yaga however is far from being your average soft-spoken grandmother. Because this witch has scared and terrorized small children across Eastern Europe for centuries. Baba Yaga’s appearances: Not a sweet grandmother. She appears to be an ugly old woman with poor hygiene. The way how someone would imagine an old hag would look like, this is an alternative version of how an old witch would look like. The facial features would consist of a huge and long distorted nose and long ugly teeth.

Chort:

A demon from Russian folklore, has an appearance as a human with goat’s features such as horns, hind legs and tail. The creature is a bad omen as it’s known to harbor misery to anybody who encounters or vandalizes its property. Chort’s appearance: Demonic, with goat hind legs, tail and horns, the skin may be covered with fur and the facial features may be similar to that of a human.

Australia

Yowie:

Though thought more to be a cryptid, there is still a tale about the Yowie. As a folklore creature, many Australian still believe in the existence of the Yowie. North America has Bigfoot and Sasquatch, as Nepal has the Yeti, then, the Yowie is Australia's unidentified bipedal creature.

Bunyip:

Is a creature from the aboriginal folklore, a creature said to reside in the swamps, creeks, riverbeds, lagoons and waterholes. The bunyip’s been depicted as amphibious, if not a hybrid of some sort. It’s uncertain to say if the bunyip has ever been known as a cryptid creature or an unknown folklore creature lurking in the swamp somewhere in southeastern Australia. Bunyip’s appearance: Amphibious-like creature with various features such as round head connects with the elongated neck and with a body which resembles an ox.

Yara-ma-yha-who:

A part of the aboriginal folklore, this creature is a blood-sucking creature that lurks in the trees. It’s expected the creature to locate on the highest top of the tree, as it waits for their prey to come by and stand under the tree before the Yara-ma-yha-who would descend down and suck the blood before swallowing the person whole.

Africa (central, south)

Kongamato:

Appears as a large cryptid pterosaur-like creature, it has gotten the name which refers to “breaker of boats” or “overturner of boats”, is because the creatures are said to have capsized and overturned the canoes as well as attacking the people who enter close by the rivers. The creatures are said to live in the regions of Zambia, Congo and Angola. Still, the creature’s existence is debatable and belongs to the unknown cryptid which is also part of Central African’s folklore. Kongamato’s appearance: Flying creature, with large wingspans and the creature resemblance a lot like the prehistoric dinosaur - pterodactyl.

If we take out the context, hearing that people enjoy dressing up as monsters and scaring each other and then call it a holiday, seems like a strange situation. However, the existence and prominence of such a tradition is completely expected when we take into consideration humanity’s collective fascination with death, myths and the overall mystery of a paranormal world.

Year after year horror movies and haunted houses are an absolute success. People voluntarily sign up to be scared and most of them enjoy the experience. When researching why we take pleasure in watching scary things or putting ourselves through a terrifying experience, the consensus was that it all comes back to control. We love being scared in an environment that we can manipulate. We can turn off that movie or decide to not go through another haunted maze. Nobody actually craves to be in serious danger, however the controlled danger we feel when indulging in such activities gives us a much needed rush of adrenaline. On top of that, it can also make us feel proud for conquering certain fears.

With an interview from a student who claims Halloween to be her favorite holiday, her opinion has strengthened the holiday even further.

“Halloween is actually my favorite holiday. I love the idea of having the freedom to be whoever or whatever you want, it allows people to show a part of their true selves. I’m also a real fan of everything that is considered scary, like haunted houses, horror movies and so on. I love the rush of adrenaline you get from being scared, it’s euphoric. And you can only enjoy that in a controlled environment, where your brain knows that nothing bad will actually happen to you.” - Flavia Bogdan

Halloween in Norway

Halloween didn’t arrive in Norway until the 2000 through Sweden, it started slow and didn’t make a breakthrough until some years later. A totally uncommon season which isn’t considered as official to the Norwegian tradition, but throughout time it has gradually become acceptable and here to stay. During recent years, this international tradition has found its way and is part of Norwegian culture.

Unlike Ireland, celebrating Halloween in Norway is practiced as a spooky activity for fun, good for marketing and commercials. Moving away from what it originally meant to be, Halloween in Norwegian style consists of a dress up party, either public or private, and has nothing to do with celebrating the homecoming of the spirits. While Halloween celebration in Norway is nonetheless short and the home decoration isn’t much of a hype. Since it’s not what the Norwegian recall it as completely official despite it being confirmed as one. Generally speaking the concept of having Halloween in Norway is more towards marketing purposes rather than the actual practice of the Celtics.

While spending a lot of effort on setting up Halloween home decoration for the occasion is normal for English-speaking countries, however, in Norway, they would spend more time on setting up Christmas holiday or preparing for Christmas decoration more so. The trend for trick-or-treating can be daunting, as some Norwegian households refuse to open the door for strangers and the sweets aren’t always available like what they are used to in England or the US. Although the celebration of Halloween isn’t part of the tradition and isn’t big in Norway, still, it's proof that this unorthodox holiday manages to break the Norwegian norms and is here to stay.

On the other hand, holidays like Halloween bring a very important thing back into this modern world, magic and wonder. Throughout the passing of time, we as people forgot to look around and enjoy the mysteries of nature around us, we stopped believing in fairytales and, as a result, stopped believing in the monsters that we once knew.

In the past, creatures were a way in which humans could explain why and how atrocities happen. It was easier to point to a ghost or an evil spirit than to admit the dangerous one might be your neighbour. Today we know that most bad things are done by the people around us but for one night it is fun to forget all about real life and take a leap into the darker side of things.
